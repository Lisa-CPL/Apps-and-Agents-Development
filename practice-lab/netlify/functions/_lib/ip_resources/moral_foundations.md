# CPL Moral Foundations Framework

## Overview

People hold their values through different moral foundations. When analyzing statements for
underlying values, consider these six foundations (based on Moral Foundations Theory):

## The Six Foundations

### 1. Care / Harm
**Core concern:** Protecting the vulnerable, preventing suffering, expressing compassion
**Indicators:** Language about welfare, safety, protection, cruelty, suffering, empathy
**Example:** "We need stronger regulations to protect workers from unsafe conditions."

### 2. Fairness / Cheating
**Core concern:** Justice, equal treatment, reciprocity, proportionality, rights
**Indicators:** Language about equality, exploitation, unfairness, being cheated, rights
**Example:** "Everyone should play by the same rules regardless of their background."

### 3. Loyalty / Betrayal
**Core concern:** Group solidarity, duty to community, trust, belonging, in-group pride
**Indicators:** Language about loyalty, community, us vs. them, tradition, betrayal, duty
**Example:** "You have to stand by your community even when it's difficult."

### 4. Authority / Subversion
**Core concern:** Respect for legitimate authority, tradition, hierarchy, order
**Indicators:** Language about order, respect, duty, roles, discipline, chaos, deference
**Example:** "Institutions exist for a reason — undermining them creates instability."

### 5. Sanctity / Degradation
**Core concern:** Purity, sacredness, disgust responses, moral cleanliness
**Indicators:** Language about degradation, purity, contamination, the sacred, the profane
**Example:** "Some things should be protected from commodification — they're sacred."

### 6. Liberty / Oppression
**Core concern:** Freedom from coercion, individual autonomy, resistance to domination
**Indicators:** Language about freedom, rights, coercion, government overreach, independence
**Example:** "People should be free to make their own choices without government interference."

## Why Moral Foundations Matter for Conversation

Recognizing moral foundations helps bridge across difference. When people disagree, they often
invoke different foundations — not just different facts. Understanding *which* foundation
underlies a view opens space for genuine curiosity rather than reflexive debate.

A person arguing for stricter immigration enforcement may be invoking Loyalty and Authority.
A person arguing for open borders may be invoking Care and Fairness. Both are drawing on real,
deeply-held values — just different ones. Recognizing this is the first step toward a different
kind of conversation.

## Applying the Framework

When identifying moral foundations:
1. Look past the surface position to the underlying value concern
2. Consider that most statements invoke more than one foundation
3. The "deepest" foundation is often the one that would cause the most distress if violated —
   not the first one named on the surface
4. Treat all foundations as legitimate — your job is to recognize them, not rank or judge them
